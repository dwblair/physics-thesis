      5. Background Theory
        1. Energy cost for creating a disclination
          1. Assume flate 2D membrane w/ Young's modulus Y, etc
          2. Ref (10), (11)
        2. Griffith criterion for spontaneous crack propagation
          1. Assume crack of length, l
          2. Potential energy of the sheet, $V$
          3. surface enrgy per unit length, $V_o$
          4. Crack of length $\ell$
          5. Crack is perpendicular to circumferential component $\sigma_{\theta \theta}$ of the disclination induced mechanical stress
          6. Potential energy of the sheet: $V =-\frac{\pi \ell^2 {\sigma_{\theta \theta}}^2 (1-\nu^2)}{4 Y} + 2 \gamma \ell + V_0$
          7. $\nu$ is the Poisson Ratio
          8. $Y$ is the Young's modulus
          9. $\gamma$ is the surface energy per unit length
          10. $V_0$ is the elastic energy in the absence of any cracks, or applied stres
        3. Minimize $V$, get:
          1. $\ell_c = \frac{ 4 Y \gamma}{\pi {\sigma_{\theta \theta}}^2 (1-\nu^2)}$
          2. Cracks with length $\ell \ge \ell_c$ will grow to lower their energy
          3. Cracks with length $\ell < \ell_c$ will heal
        4. 'Hoop stress': $\sigma_{\theta \theta}$
          1. Hoop stress causes cracks to open up
          2. Obtain it from Airy stress function $\chi(r)$ \cite{seung} at a distance $r$ from a positive disclination at the center of a two dimensional membrane of radius $R$
          3. $\chi(r) = \frac{Y s}{8 \pi} r^2 \left ( \ln \frac{r}{R} - \frac{1}{2} \right )$
          4. The hoop stress is the circumferential component of the stress tensor $\sigma$
          5. Given by $\sigma_{\theta \theta}= \frac{\partial^2 \chi}{\partial r^2}= \frac{Y}{12} \left(1 + \ln \frac{r}{R} \right )$.
        5. When critical crack length is ~= a lattice spacing, even a single disclination can rupture crystallite.
        6. Substituting $\sigma_{\theta \theta}$ in expression for criticla crack size, we get:
          1. $\ell_c = \frac{ 4 Y \gamma 144}{\pi (1-\nu^2) Y^2 (1+ \ln \frac{r}{R})^2} \approx \frac{576 \gamma}{\pi Y}$
          2. assuming $\nu^2 << 1$ and $r \sim R$
          3. So, when $Y >> \gamma$, the prob'l'y of the crystallite rupturing is greater.
        7. Estimation of $Y$ and $\gamma$ for our system
          1. $Y = - \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} U^{''}(r)|_{r=a}$
          2. where $a$ is equilibrium separation between the particles forming the cluster
          3. consider a hexagonal cluster with each side of dimension $M a$
          4. distance of an interfacial line from the center of mass of the cluster is proportional to the interfacial energy of this line
          5. Therefore, $\gamma M \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} a = 6 M U(a)$ becomes $\gamma = \frac{4\sqrt{3} U(a)}{a}$
          6. So, critical length $\ell_c \approx \frac{- 576 \times 6}{\pi a} \frac{U(a)}{U''(a)}$
        8. Resulting predictions:
          1. for the 'depletion' potential, $l_c=0.35 a$
          2. for screened coloumb, for the potential in Eq.(\ref{potential-brittleductile}), $l_c \approx \frac{1100}{a} \frac{\lambda^2 (a-\sigma)}{-a+\sigma+2\lambda}$ where $a=\lambda+\sigma$
          3. when $\sigma=1$ and $\lambda=0.2$, the critical crack length is very large: $l_c \approx 30.6 a$
          4. when $\lambda=0.014$, the critical crack length is a fraction of the lattice spacing, {\it viz}, $l_c \approx 0.21a$
          5. Only a single net disclination required to rupture cluster for short-range potential
        9. the energy required to introduce a disclination at the center of the crystallite is $E \approx 0.0014 N U_0 (\lambda + \sigma)^2/\lambda^2$, for the potential in Eq.\ref{potential-brittleductile}
        10. cost of introducing a disclination is $\propto 1/\lambda^2$ for $\sigma >> \lambda$
        11. this cost increases reapidly with decreasing potential range
        12. suggests the existence of a lower bound on the range of the potential for thermal activation of disclinations
        13. These two competing effects imply that the crossover in the melting rate can arise due to the presence of disclinations only at an optimum range of values for the range of the inter-particle interaction potential

    2. Methods
      1. Swendesen-Wang Algorithm
        1. SW algorithm \cite{SwWA} used to generate statistics for models, create the bond-paths studied here
        2. Based on work of Fortuin and Kasteleyn \cite{FoKa}
        3. Procedure:
          1. Introduce bonds with probability $p(\sigma_i,\sigma_j) = \delta_{\sigma_i, \sigma_j} (1-e^{-K})$
          2. Create clusters of bonded spins
          3. Choose one of $q$ possible spin states and assign to all sites in the cluster
        4. Reduces critical slowing relative to algorithms that flip individaul spins \cite{NeBa99}, e.g. Metropolis algoirithm \cite{Met}
        5. Bonds introduced in SW algorithm correspond to correlations among spins
        6. We study paths along bonds in these clusters
      2. Determining the Chem Distance and Diameter
        1. Review of Previous methods
          1. Stanley, Grassberger \cite{Gr99}, Leath, Paul \cite{Paul2001}, etc.
          2. Memory considerations, two seeds, etc.
        2. Leath growth \cite{Leath}
          1. using a random number generator, one assigns all the bonds associated with the seed site the status ``occupied'' or ``unoccupied'' with probability $p$
          2. If a bond is assigned ``occupied'' status, the site to which this bond connects is deemed a ``growth site'', and is added to cluster.
          3. All the sites thus added to the cluster in this round form a ``chemical shell'' of distance $l$ from the seed site.
          4. This process is then continued for subsequent generations of growth trials, each associated with a larger chemical shell; the growth process stops naturally when one of the growth rounds generates no new growth sites.
          5. (Note: sites not added to the cluster in a particular round get another chance to be added to the cluster in subsequent rounds; but, once added, are no longer considered as possible growth sites.)
        3. Leath growth most appropriate for what we're measuring
          1. Can't use two-seed method; we must find all possible paths
      3. Procedure for $q>1$
        1. Generate a new cluster configuration using the Swendsen-Wang algorithm (see above) with periodic boundary conditions. The identification of connected clusters in this steps allows us to determine the largest cluster in the system.
        2. Choose a random site $s$ on this cluster as the seed site.
        3. Beginning with the seed site $s$, determine all sites in the largest cluster by ``growing'' along satisfied cluster bonds (this process does not change the bonds that were determined in step 1).
        4. The chemical shell reached in the final step of this growth process, $shell_{final}$, is considered to be the randomly-chosen chemical distance on the largest critical cluster, and is added to our statistics for the chemical distance.
        5. All the $i$ sites at the end of this growth process whose nearest neighbors are all occupied are deemed to be perimeter sites, $p_i$. This set includes all of the external perimeter sites of the cluster.
        6. A similar Leath growth process is preformed using each of the perimeter sites as seeds, and ${shell_{final}}_i$ from each of these growth processes is stored.
        7. The diameter for the largest cluster is then $max\{{shell_{final}}_i\}$
        8. This method for finding the diameter is an improvement over the naive $N^2$ algorithm for solving the all-pairs maximum shortest path problem on the paths formed along cluster bonds. It is expected to scale as $O(pN)$, where $p$ is the number of perimeter sites on the largest critical cluster.
      4. Procedure for $q>1$
        1. For $q=1$, it is possible to grow a cluster from a seed site.
        2. Diameter must have its endpoints on perimeter sites
        3. Any ``pins'', or singly-connected paths on the external perimeter of the cluster, contain sites that can be eliminated as possible diameter endpoints
        4. Straightforward to show that the existence of such a ``pin'' also allows us to eliminate as candidate diameter endpoints that lie within the ``body'' of the cluster as well
        5. 'Proof' of / argument for the algorithm:
          1. $P$: the set of all sites on the pin $P$
          2. let $p_{tip}$: the site that is the outermost tip of a given pin (i.e., the site with only one nearest neighbor) and $p_{attach}$ the site that attaches this pin to the body of the cluster (i.e., a site with more than 2 nearest neighbors)
          3. Imagine that we were to include as a candidate site in $S$ some site from $P$ that was not $p_{tip}$, resulting in a candidate diameter $D'$; it would be immediately clear that rejecting this site in favor of $p_{tip}$ would result in a new candidate diameter $D''>D'$. We can therefore exclude all sites in in $P$ that are closer than $p_{tip}$ to $S$.
          4. (?) Similar considerations (PROVE THIS?) allow us to additionally exclude from $S$ all sites in $N$ that have a chemical distance from $p_{attach}$ less than or equal to the chemical distance between $p_{tip}$ and $p_{attach}$ (i.e., the length of the pin).
          5. Initiate, for every site i$s$ in $S$, a ``Leath growth'' search that examines the chemical distance between along the cluster between $s$ and every other site on the cluster, terminating when all cluster sites have been examined.
          6. The maximum chemical distance found across all such searches is then $D$.
          7. We thus need only consider a relatively small proportion [quantify this proportion, on average] of cluster sites as possible diameter endpoints, greatly reducing the number of ``Leath scans'' required in order to determine the diameter exactly
          8. Note that this method does not work for periodic boundary conditions, however; we must therefore grow clusters from a seed site, retaining only those clusters that do not grow to touch the boundaries of the lattice.
        6. Procedure
          1. Choose a growth seed site in the center of the lattice
          2. Perform a Leath growth from this site until the cluster dies, or reaches the boundaries of the maximum lattice size of $L_{max}$. If any cluster site borders $L_{max}$, begin again at step 1.
          3. Identify all the perimeter sites in the cluster by choosing all sites in the final growth step that are perimeter sites (i.e., those that have less than the maximum number of allowed nearest neighbors). In this geometry, all the sites in the final chemical shell will be external perimeter sites.
          4. Identify all the ``pins'' among these perimeter sites by performing a Leath growth from each pin site until one finds a site that is not singly-connected. All of the sites in the ``neck'' of the pin are eliminated from consideration as diameter endpoints.
          5. Beginning from the point of attachment of the pin to the body of the cluster, continue the Leath scan until one has achieved a chemical shell equal to the distance (along sites) between the point of attachment and the end of the pin. All of sites thus scanned are also eliminated from consideration as diameter endpoints.
          6. Perform Leath growths from all of the remaining perimeter sites $p_i$, collecting the maximum chemical shells reached in each instance; the largest of these chemical shells is then the diameter of the cluster.

        1. We used the 'blocking' method \cite{NeBa99} to extract the proper standard deviation for chemical distance and diameter from our measurements.

        3. $\sigma=\sqrt{ \frac{< m^2 > - < m > ^2}{n-1}}$, where $n$ is the number of blocks
